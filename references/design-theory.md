# Identity Design Theory and Best Practices

Durable theory that outlives trends. Use during VI distillation and when
judging generated candidates.

## Strategy before marks

- **Derive the brand IP from the business scenario, not from incidental
  metaphors.** The mark must abstract what the product *does or means for
  its buyers* — not a phrase from internal docs, the agent's process
  language, or the first material metaphor at hand. Test: explain the mark
  to a category buyer in one sentence that mentions the product's job.
- **Kapferer's Brand Identity Prism** (physique, personality, culture,
  relationship, reflection, self-image) is the standard pre-design strategy
  artifact — https://www.mindtools.com/awqj2p3/kapferers-brand-identity-prism/
- **Brand architecture** (Aaker/Joachimsthaler Brand Relationship Spectrum:
  branded house / house of brands / endorsed / sub-brands) decides whether
  the mark is a master-brand, endorsed, or standalone mark — ask this before
  drawing. https://www.appinio.com/en/blog/market-research/brand-architecture
- **Neumeier (The Brand Gap, Zag):** radical differentiation; name and
  expression judged on memorability, extendibility, depth.

## What a mark is for: distinctive brand assets (DBA)

Jenni Romaniuk (Ehrenberg-Bass): non-name assets (logos, colors, shapes,
characters, sonic cues) exist to trigger the brand in buyers' memory,
measured on **Fame** (% who link the asset to you) and **Uniqueness**
(% for whom it points only to you). Implications:

- Assets take years of consistent use to build; do not refresh like fashion.
- Meaning is built by consistent pairing, not chosen off a color-psychology
  infographic — universal color-emotion mappings have weak, culture-bound
  evidence (red serves both Coca-Cola and Wells Fargo; Kandinsky's
  color-shape correspondences fail empirical testing).
- Launch backlash is a poor predictor of long-term equity (Bierut: logos
  are "empty vessels" that acquire meaning — Gap 2010 vs Obama 2008).
- Reference: https://marketingscience.info/news-and-insights/qa-seven-costly-sins-of-brand-identity

## Craft criteria

- **Haviv's three-criterion test** (Chermayeff & Geismar & Haviv):
  *appropriate, distinctive/memorable, simple.* "Not about what one likes:
  about what works." A mark may need to be unusual, even awkward, to hook
  memory. Design in pencil / black-and-white first; color is the last layer.
- **Wordmark vs symbol vs monogram** is strategic: wordmarks for short
  distinctive names and new brands needing name awareness; monograms for
  long names and tiny surfaces; standalone symbols need justification
  (long name, entity consolidation, visual shorthand — Chase, NBC).
- **Gestalt mechanics:** closure (WWF panda), figure-ground/negative space
  (FedEx arrow), similarity (Olympic rings' uniform stroke). One silhouette,
  one signal.
- **Grid skepticism:** golden-ratio overlay diagrams are largely
  post-rationalization (the Apple golden-ratio construction is debunked;
  Janoff drew it freehand). Grids are for optical refinement, not proof of
  quality; successful marks are often deliberately not geometrically precise.

## Verification battery (apply to every candidate)

1. **Shrink test:** legible at 32 px; survives 16 px favicon; key elements
   ≥ ~6 px at minimum size; ship a simplified small-size variant if needed.
2. **Squint test:** silhouette recognizable when blurred / at distance.
3. **Black-and-white-first:** the mark must work with color removed.
4. **Crop test:** survives square app-icon and circular avatar crops.
5. **Contrast compliance:** brand color pairings meet WCAG 2.2 AA
   (4.5:1 normal text, 3:1 large text/UI); prefer APCA (Lc ≥ 75 for body
   text) for dark-mode palettes. Emit palettes as verified fg/bg *pairs*,
   not loose swatches. https://colorarchive.org/guides/color-contrast-accessibility-guide/
6. **One-sentence test:** the mark's idea can be stated in one sentence
   grounded in the business scenario.

## Verbal identity

Roughly half of a real guidelines document is verbal: naming, voice, tone,
messaging. Voice doesn't change; tone adapts to the reader's state of mind.
The canonical open reference is Mailchimp's Content Style Guide
(CC-licensed, https://github.com/mailchimp/content-style-guide). A complete
brand kit should state at minimum: name usage rules, one-line voice
description, and 3–5 tone rules for product copy.
